Many people are very familiar with Christian history and writings, but a surprising number of people who call themselves Christian actually know very little about the origins of the Christian faith.

Okay, maybe not fair to say the "origins', because every religion has been begot to some extent by those that came before.

One thing that traditional mainstream Protestants may be unaware of is the wealth of literature that exists that was not accepted by what came to be the Church of the Eastern/Western Roman Empire.  I was going to put a few links here, but realized the reader can search Google as well as I.  Very few books actually made it into the canon, and there were a large variety of sects and interpretations regarding who Jesus was, what he taught, and what it meant to be a Christian.

The first literature that  was written down by Christians were the epistles, some of which came to be incorporated into the New Testament.  These began to be circulated between 50-70 AD.  There were also pre-gospel texts being circulated around the same time.  Closer to the turn of the first century, gospels began to be written regarding the birth, life, and death of Jesus.  There are no corroborating sources of Jesus' historical existence outside of Christian literature. There is a very interesting website out there about how this religion may have come into being without the physical reality of Jesus of Nazareth.

The second century saw a more active struggle for control over the official doctrine.  Many teachings were circulated, those of Origen, Tertullian, and the Manicheans and other forms of Gnosticism, to name a very few.  Again, this isn't a treatise, and you can find this information at your local library or on the Internet.

The Nicene Creed was formulated in 325 AD, and most of mainstream Christianity adheres to it's basic principles.  The exception, is that the Eastern Orthodox Churches object to the following line:

We believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life,
who proceeds from the Father and the Son.

The Orthodox Churches believe that both the Son and the Holy Spirit proceed from the Father, who is the fountainhead of the Trinity.

I stop here, because I wanted to establish that Christianity has never been a united religion in which all Christians understood, believed and practiced the same thing.  It was in the 4th century that it became formulated, and those who considered themselves with the most political power declared those who did not accept their canon and interpretation as being "heretics".  It was around this time that the Eastern Churches began to see themselves as teaching "Orthodox" teachings, or "right teachings", and that those outside of their interpretation were supposedly in error.

Many people have been influenced, for good or evil, by teachings of this religion, but it has always been evolving and open to personal experience and interpretation.  As individuals, we are free to read, learn, interpret, and practice, according to the Spirit's revelation to us.
