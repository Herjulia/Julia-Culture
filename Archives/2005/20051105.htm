Wisdom Walk

07/26/2004 8:51 AM

I have been doing some thinking about spirituality as a "path of wisdom".  So I decided to hash out some of my own ideas about wisdom.
I would define wisdom as a humble, compassionate, insightful intelligence.  Webster's dictionary defines it as "soundness of judgment", but I think that the soundness comes from the other three components I've included in my definition.  Allow me to expound.

Why is humility important?

First, another definition.  From the American Heritage Dictionary, the first definition is what I'd like to describe: "Marked by meekness or modesty in behavior, attitude, or spirit; not arrogant or prideful."

Or my own:  an honest and accurate self-evaluation.  I am not describing a servile or weak attitude here;  I believe someone can make significant "boasts" and still be humble if the boast is an accurate description of their skill or self.  And this would also exclude a false humility, that would downgrade one's talents in order to maintain an outward appearance of meekness, or to avoid taking on the actions or discipline that talent might demand.

The opposite, of course, would be any inaccurate, dishonest, or blurred self-evaluation.  I seen these conditions as a problem because they are weak.  As I mentioned, if we engage in false humility, we don't live up to our potential.  If we engage in arrogance, we are blind to our faults, and that is where another can take advantage of us, or we get blindsided by events in our lives.

Why is compassion important?

I begin by defining my terms again.  "Deep awareness of the suffering of another coupled with the wish to relieve it. ", is the definition given in the American Heritage Dictionary.  Mine is a definition that I think sprouts from humility--the forgiving of other's weaknesses because you know your own frailty.  I see compassion as being a little more mundane, rather than waiting for deep suffering.  Compassion is roused when we see other's simple struggles, as well as when something tragic happens.

Why is that important to wisdom?  It allows for connection and harmony with other spirits.  It inspires cooperative effort. It opens us to learning from others, directly.  And it opens us to learning by entering into their suffering.

Why is insight important?
 
More from American Heritage: "

  1. The capacity to discern the true nature of a situation; penetration.
  2. The act or outcome of grasping the inward or hidden nature of things or of perceiving in an intuitive manner."
    3.

I like them both, and don't need to add anything.  But why is it important?  Again, the more blind we are, the less likely we are to base our actions on a solid foundation, the more likely to stumble.

Lastly, intelligence.

Of course.  What would wisdom be without facts, details, reality, to frame our decisions around?

So what would a wisdom walk look like?

A path of wisdom would increase humility, compassion, insight, and intelligence.  But how would you go about doing it?

It seems to me that to develop humility means, not merely taking a lesser role sometimes, but also testing our limits.  What other way to discover our capabilities or boundaries, than by pushing ourselves to where we can no longer perform?  Often we are tempted to avoid an activity because we don't think we would be good at it, but this is pride, the fear of failing.  Failing is good, because then we know where we are at.

Compassion for me has been aroused most often when I choose to rub elbows with those who suffer.  I am not talking about becoming the support for every needy person who comes along.  But for instance, in a role as a respiratory therapist working in a hospital, I have good boundaries, but am interacting with people in their suffering.  This could be done in any setting, provided you decided to take on the mindset of identifying with the people around you, listening to them, and wondering what you would feel like.  Allowing yourself to be affected by their troubles, at least to some degree.  Keep the boundaries, but remember your frailty and at least feel for others.

Insight comes to us naturally sometimes, but can be cultivated through meditation, reflection, journaling, pondering, and debating, to name a few activities.  For me, discussion groups online are extremely helpful, as others bring in perspectives I just don't come up with on my own.  Again, sometimes we avoid these "conflicts" because they make us uncomfortable, but that goes back to humility and the need to push our limits.  Don't be afraid to look like a fool if you stand to gain more insight.

Lastly, intelligence.  How often do we formulate an opinion without putting in the legwork of actually studying the subject?  It's easy to do, and at least in America, all too prevalant.  I have noticed among the pagan community a great emphasis on scholarship, and I am excited and inspired by it.  So find the facts you can, and see what you can verify or prove.  Temper it by understanding it's limitations, applying it by identifying with others, and penetrate beyond it's surface.


Spiritual "dating"

08/11/2004 07:58 AM -0500

'What?  Like "carbon dating"?  You want to bring spirituality into your dating?  I thought you were in a committed relationship?'

Now simmer down.  I had this "aha" moment today, when I realized there is a dimension of my spiritual exploration that is like dating.  In my case, like guys.  So I will be adding the "adolescent crush" analysis to the cerebral and analytical information on each spiritual topic.

DISCLAIMER:  this part is in no way intended to be prescriptive or to say this is how I feel through and through.  I'm attempting to extrovert some of my sillier and less rational "stuff" so I can get at the core of what I really am looking for spiritually.

Spiritual paths and the mindset that follows

The one thing I have found all spiritual paths seem to have in common, is that whether the adherents acknowledge it or not, they think they have the edge on spiritual truth.  Even those who proclaim vehemently that they have nothing against other religions or spiritual traditions, or that there path is not religious, just spiritual, and that they are open to the spiritual experience of any person.  If you start talking to them, before long, it will become apparent that they view you as needing to be brought up to speed on "real" spiritual wisdom, and that it can really be found by doing what they do.

I guess by wandering around and asking a lot of questions, I always feel like my point of view is open to debate.  I hope that feeling keeps me from this kind of spiritual arrogance.  I hope it keeps me able to hear wise words when they are offered, no matter who they come from.

I do read books--but websites are so much  more accessible.  One that gave a lot of good information about the Mystery Religions is here: Christianity & the "Mystery Religions"  .  This gentlemen has a lot of Christian and Medieval history information.  Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. , also wrote a paper on this subject, The Influence of the Mystery Religions on Christianity  .  And for a view that has nothing to do with Christianity, Mystery Religions Page .

Basically, many of these secret religions predated Christianity by thousands of years, and its clear they influenced the Christian faith.

My Own, SuperImportant, AllEncompassing definition of Spirituality

"We are all spiritual beings.  For me, spirituality is the combination of our bodies, minds, and subconscious, and whatever state these currently are in.  This definition acknowledges the innate spirituality of anyone, from sage to psychotic, from infant to octogenarian.

    "To me, the highest purpose in any religion is to harmonize our spirituality.  I believe we are at peace when we don't dissociate our minds from our bodies, or from our subconscious.  This is why running, dancing, or martial arts, are often described by participants in spiritual terms, because when they perform these activities they experience harmony of mind, body, and spirit.  Singing can also do this, art, and sometimes writing. (Though for me, writing tends to get me very analytical sometimes.)  This is why I think ritual is important, and when done with the intent for this kind of self-integration. Ritual taps the subconscious in the same way that art does, bringing subconscious thought into concrete action.

    "The problem with religion is when it gets politicized.  Anytime a practice, revelatory text, or spiritual  concept become the 'property' of an elite group, who define themselves as the keepers of it, or prescribe how it should be implemented or interpreted, and by whom, it becomes politicized.  And all too often, those elite incite their followers through xenophobia in order to perpetuate their own politico-religious structure.

    "So two destructive phenomenon result:  First, individuals begin to look to something outside themselves for spiritual validation, and become more and more disconnected from their innate spirituality.  Second, society becomes dissociated from this harmony, and fails to listen to the wisdom that comes naturally to us all."

Polytheism
