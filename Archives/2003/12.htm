##  2003-12-06T01:08:00Z

My unconversion

Good morning.  My kids are asleep. . .I think.  We'll see how long this lasts.

What a weird year for me!  What a rich year!  I was raised with a variety of Christian influences, being raised Catholic, having parents who got heavily involved in the Charismatic Movement of the 1970s, being dragged into a Para church organization that resembled the Branch Davidians, then went to a Christian Fundamentalist High School (this was my own 16 year old choice, based on the warped religious ideas I was immersed in).

Post high school I started to explore my own ideas, and rejected the degree of fundamentalism I was influenced by.  I explored Buddhism--for a short time even sought to be a student of Roshi Philip Kapleau (Zen: Dawn in the West; The Three Pillars of Zen); I read Drawing Down the Moon by Margot Adler, and Spiral Dance by StarHawk.  I also immersed myself in Church History, from both Church and secular sources.

For 17 years I struggled with guilt, wanting Christianity to be true.  Then this past May, I recommitted my life to Christ.  I read the Bible daily and kept a spiritual journal.  I believed that the apostolic churches were the only ones that demonstrated a historical connection and continuity between the apostles and today.  I spent about two months going to an Antiochian Orthodox Church, and sought spiritual guidance from a local Orthodox priest.  I was certain that I was going to convert, and was eager to absorb as much Orthodox teaching as I could.

In the meantime, the content and type of rituals I was being exposed to were bringing up feelings and memories, and I realized I was recreating some aspects of my earlier childhood, so that I could begin a healing process.  After really acknowledging some intense rage I had toward different events in my childhood, I woke up one morning "done" with the Christian "thing".

No one could have been more bewildered by this than I!  I took my journaling/devotions time, and researched the writing of Bertrand Russell, and read The Age of Reason by Thomas Paine.  After that, I started exploring The Jesus Puzzle.

I have been recently getting into Belief.net, asking questions about prayer/meditation/spellwork, and about spirit guides.  I also read different articles on a variety of religious ideas and traditions.  I have also been reading A History or Pagan Europe, by Prudence Jones and Nigel Pennick.

I am really freeing myself from a lot of fetters, and I have never felt so young.

    "We are all spiritual beings.  For me, spirituality is the combination of our bodies, minds, and subconscious, and whatever state these currently are in.  This definition acknowledges the innate spirituality of anyone, from sage to psychotic, from infant to octogenarian.

    "To me, the highest purpose in any religion is to harmonize our spirituality.  I believe we are at peace when we don't dissociate our minds from our bodies, or from our subconscious.  This is why running, dancing, or martial arts, are often described by participants in spiritual terms, because when they perform these activities they experience harmony of mind, body, and spirit.  Singing can also do this, art, and sometimes writing. (Though for me, writing tends to get me very analytical sometimes.)  This is why I think ritual is important, and when done with the intent for this kind of self-integration. Ritual taps the subconscious in the same way that art does, bringing subconscious thought into concrete action.

    "The problem with religion is when it gets politicized.  Anytime a practice, revelatory text, or spiritual  concept become the 'property' of an elite group, who define themselves as the keepers of it, or prescribe how it should be implemented or interpreted, and by whom, it becomes politicized.  And all too often, those elite incite their followers through xenophobia in order to perpetuate their own politico-religious structure.

    "So two destructive phenomenon result:  First, individuals begin to look to something outside themselves for spiritual validation, and become more and more disconnected from their innate spirituality.  Second, society becomes dissociated from this harmony, and fails to listen to the wisdom that comes naturally to us all."

Man, I guess I'm all over the place with this religion stuff.  The History of Pagan Europe really ignited my thinking.  In the past, I always bought into what I was raised with, the idea that Roman, Greek, and Norse mythology is just a collection of stories and legends, but the Bible, now that's real.  And also, that it is silly to believe in many gods, because to be God, one has to be all knowing, all powerful, yada yada .....

So this obvious thought got through my thick skull:  why is it superior to believe that One God is mysteriously manifest in Three Persons, but not that One God is mysteriously manifest in a multitude of deities, or even, in human vs. animal manifestation?  So I realized that there is nothing superior to it, and that Christianity is not a monotheistic religion.  (I know, any Jews reading this want to knock me upside the head--"what do you think we've been saying. . .")

Anyway, this thinking, plus a curiosity about my pre-Christian Northern European ancestors, has taken me to quite a few Asatru and Northern Heathenism sites.

Then a weird thing happened at work.  After spending a few minutes looking at some Heathen sites, and reading a quote from The Troth:  "The old gods are calling; to hear their call is a joy, an honor, and also a duty. If they are calling to you, it is time to take your place among friends and kin, and bring new honor and strength to our ancient Heathen faith."  After reading this, I got off the Internet, went to email, then opened the program that does our billing.  As I was logging on, there was a message between the computer address and the login command:  "gl the gods are calling".  Could my Admin accomplish that?  I mean, were they watching me, and put that in the UNIX program when I logged in?  Or is it just a message between the IT guys, and I "coincidentally" came across it.  I suppose I could ask our IT department.
