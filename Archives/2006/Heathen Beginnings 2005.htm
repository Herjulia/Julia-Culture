Friday, April 29, 2005

I have decided to commit to a Heathen path.

This will definately be as a solitary for now, although I have contacted the local "meet up" group, just for people to rub elbows with.

I should record how I finally have settled on this.

I guess this need was sparked when I first visited ScareSpite. His emphasis on the land, and contacting the ancestors within, moved me.

Then of course, I rubbed elbows with the various Heathen on the Bnet Shamanism board, and found that I just plain liked talking to them.

But I was weirded out by the god/desses thing, and needed to work through that.

And then, there was this journey, where I was told to look to my Roman ancestors. After looking into the relationship between Hera and her peacock, I thought maybe that's what I was supposed to go for. But I explored a little more of Hellenismos, and Religio Roma, and found it to structured, somewhat dualistic, and definately too subservient for my tastes. I just couldn't see it, and went back in journey to respectfully decline the invitation.

So I was walking last week, and I just knew. I don't know all the lore, I don't know even much ritual. I just knew this is so like me I can hardly see it. It's so close, I don't realize it's there. I don't know how to put that into words. There's a way of life that is IN me, and I'm most in harmony when I'm "hanging out" with Heathens.

So I am going to take baby steps, and make my spirituality one with this path. And be nourished by it.

picking up in stanza 14, Fafnir is lying on the Gnita Heath in the shape of a dragon, with the Helm of Terror.

From the Viking Answer Lady--

The name of this symbol is ægishjálmr, which when literally translated means "helm of awe" or "helm of terror". It is used in various places in the sagas, where it can have many other meanings, such as "countenance of terror" or "overbearing nature".

The term ægishjálmr probably did not refer to a real, physical helmet originally, but rather originated in the use of a special kind of magic called seiðr. Seiðr could be used to affect the mind with forgetfulness, delusion, illusion, or fear. The ægishjálmr is a special subset of seiðr magic called sjónhverfing, the magical delusion or "deceiving of the sight" where the seið-witch affects the minds of others so that they cannot see things as they truly are. The role of seiðr in illusion magic is well-documented in the sagas, particularly being used to conceal a person from his pursuers. Part of this power may have been due to hypnosis, for the seið-witch could be deprived of her powers by being deprived of her sight, and the effect faded when the victim left the presence of the seið-practitioner.
