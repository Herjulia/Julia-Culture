Home Birth

It isn't as bad as it's opponents would have you think.

It isn't as glorious as it's proponents would have you think.

After being a Respiratory Therapist for 9 years, I was very aware of how our medical community can exaggerate situations at times.  Don't get me wrong; medical science has advanced healthcare in a multitude of ways.  And when it is implemented with a scientific attitude, it can be a great thing.

But when it's implemented in the following ways, it goes awry: For ego-- whether doctor, nurse, RT, or whoever, thinking they are personally responsible for the outcome of each medical situation; For teaching purposes--the moments when the decision to perform a procedure is influenced by the need to train a resident in that procedure, rather than it's true necessity; For pharmaceutical or equipment testing, exploration, justification--when we become fascinated with all of our various modalities, and the control we imagine we have, or are influenced by their advertising and economic needs to implement their products; For the adrenaline rush--medical people often thrive on stress, and a certain traumatic air, and at times, we create those situations by our mentality, and sort of a self-fulfilling prophecy.

So I investigated home birthing.  I knew several women over the years who had great things to say about their home birth experience.  I read several books, looked at what the World Health Organization has to say about birthing, and interviewed probably 5 women who have had multiple home births.  Asked a lot of questions of my certified nurse midwives regarding medical situations I had witnessed in birth, and was satisfied with how they said they would handle these outcomes.

Connie Marie was born at home after 60 hours of labor, 5 hours of pushing, and probably about a 500cc blood loss.  It was back labor:  I spent probably 2 solid days feeling like I was being crushed in a garbage truck.  People say "Why didn't you go to the hospital after labor wasn't progressing?"  Well, one, my midwives kept assuring me that I was going to deliver very soon; two, the thought of getting into an ambulance and riding seemed like it would be too painful (I know, when you're rational, you're thinking how could it really have been MORE painful?); three, I know the medical community, and they have their own prejudices--I didn't want to deal with them trying to "fix" what I had "screwed up", or subconsciously punish me for making what they would consider bad medical choices.

I started my child's infancy off with absolutely no energy to give her.  She had colic, I struggled through breastfeeding, and was profoundly depressed.

York Joseph was born in an absolutely wonderful hospital, Annapolis Hospital in Wayne, MI.  It's a little community hospital, and apparently only one team that can do epidurals.  They were in a C-section when I was delivering, so I was given a narcotic called Stadol.  I hallucinated during the delivery, screamed my fool head off, saw Noah's ark and his animals running around while I was trying to have a baby.  After delivery, I had no trouble nursing, and the baby was not obtunded in any way. The nursing staff was supportive, not controlling.  They really respected what I was aiming at, and were there for me, but also gave me a lot of space.

Hallucinating beat the hell out of home birthing.
